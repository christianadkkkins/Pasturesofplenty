# Preprint Results And Discussion: LTST Gram-Memory Taxonomy v2

## Results

We evaluated the projective O(d) Gram-memory feature set on the completed 86-record LTST cohort using the previously assigned regime labels collapsed into three phenotype targets: `loose_orbit` (`angle_only` and `angle_first`, n=19), `constrained_orbit` (`long_only`, `long_first`, and `both_same_horizon`, n=41), and `rigid_orbit` (`neither`, n=26). The analysis used the first 500 non-ST beats of each record as the baseline window and summarized the degree of temporal configuration collapse among the present state, a short-memory EMA, and a long-memory EMA. Across the cohort, the strongest separation was observed between `loose_orbit` records and the remaining records. In particular, `loose_orbit` records showed lower present-to-history projective line lock and higher residual transverse freedom than the high-stiffness families: median `proj_line_lock_xl` was 0.9498 in `loose_orbit` versus 0.9823 in `constrained_orbit` and 0.9798 in `rigid_orbit`, while median `proj_transverse_xl` was 0.0502, 0.0177, and 0.0202, respectively. Baseline projective volume and temporal area measures were the strongest `loose_orbit` discriminators. The best one-vs-rest AUROC was obtained by the baseline p95 of normalized temporal 3-cell volume (`baseline_p95_temporal_volume_3`, AUROC 0.738), followed by baseline p95 temporal area (`baseline_p95_temporal_area_loss`, AUROC 0.715); baseline median log volume and baseline median normalized temporal volume both reached AUROC 0.704. These findings support the interpretation that angle-dominant records occupy a less collapsed temporal configuration at baseline than the non-loose records.

In contrast, the same baseline geometry did not cleanly separate `rigid_orbit` from `constrained_orbit`. Within the high-stiffness subset, the strongest `rigid_orbit` vs `constrained_orbit` discriminators were not baseline projective coordinates but ST-relative change features, and even these were weak. The best AUROC was 0.590 for `temporal_volume_3_st_abs_relative_change`, followed by 0.588 for `present_lock_barrier_st_over_baseline` and 0.586 for `temporal_area_loss_st_abs_relative_change`. Baseline present-to-history features such as `baseline_median_present_transverse_freedom`, `baseline_median_present_lock_barrier`, and `baseline_median_proj_line_lock_xl` remained close to chance for this split (AUROCs approximately 0.53-0.57). A leave-one-record-out nearest-centroid classifier built from the primary baseline summary vector further confirmed that the baseline memory geometry did not support a stable three-class taxonomy: macro-F1 was 0.2815 and macro balanced accuracy was 0.3393, with 11/19 `loose_orbit` records classified correctly, 18/41 `constrained_orbit` records classified correctly, and 0/26 `rigid_orbit` records recovered as a coherent class. Examination of the per-record summaries showed that `rigid_orbit` is itself heterogeneous, ranging from highly locked examples such as `s20141` (`proj_line_lock_xl` 0.9959, `proj_transverse_xl` 0.0041) and `s30742` (0.9932, 0.0068) to substantially less collapsed cases such as `s20331` (0.2896, 0.7104) and `s20191` (0.7704, 0.2296). Thus, the projective baseline geometry supports a robust `loose` versus non-`loose` distinction, but does not justify treating all `neither` records as a single rigid manifold.

## Discussion

The main implication of the full-cohort analysis is that projective temporal degeneration is a real cardiac signal, but it is not the whole cardiac taxonomy. Framed in projective terms, the present beat, short-memory state, and long-memory state can be viewed as a temporal line, plane, and 3-cell whose collapse is measured by normalized Gram determinants rather than raw Euclidean scale. Under that view, the `loose_orbit` phenotype is clearly identifiable: these records preserve more transverse freedom and more temporal phase volume at baseline, and therefore appear less locked to their own recent past. That result is strong enough to support a positive claim in the preprint. The negative result is equally important: the high-stiffness population does not collapse into a single `rigid_orbit` geometry. The failure of baseline projective features to recover `rigid_orbit` as a coherent class, together with the wide spread of per-record present-to-history lock values inside the `neither` group, indicates that the apparent misses are heterogeneous rather than uniformly overconstrained. In other words, the current baseline geometry identifies one clean family (`loose`) and one mixed residual family (the non-loose records), not a stable three-phenotype partition.

This reframes the next step. The projective bundle should be retained as the preferred baseline representation because it captures a scale-free notion of temporal configuration collapse and outperforms older cosine-only summaries for the `loose` family, but the unresolved problem is now within the high-stiffness subset. The most plausible interpretation is that `constrained` and `rigid` are not separated by baseline collapse magnitude alone, but by a second mechanism such as excursion persistence, event-coupled reactivity, or a still-unresolved subtype structure inside the current `neither` bucket. For the preprint, that is a more honest and more interesting claim than forcing a three-class taxonomy that the full cohort does not support. It also fits the broader cross-domain picture. In our completed solar layered run, `solar_hmm` moved before 546/589 storms with minute-level specificity 0.756, while the onset-gated LTST transition-HMM was visible before 7524/8000 ST episodes with beat-level lead-aware specificity 0.671. Those detector results do not erase the domain differences, but they do reinforce the central cross-domain point: precursor geometry is real, while the alarm policy built on top of it remains system specific. The solar layered work is summarized in [solar_report.md](runs/solar_20260408T051653Z/solar_report.md), and the completed LTST detector run is summarized in [ltst_transition_hmm_report.md](runs/ltst_full_86_20260405T035505Z/transition_hmm_onset_cooldown32/ltst_transition_hmm_report.md). Taken together, the cardiac and solar results support a domain-general geometric precursor framework with domain- and phenotype-conditioned routing, rather than a single universal class boundary learned from one system.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, the completed 86-record LTST study supports a narrow but robust claim: scale-free geometric descriptors of temporal configuration collapse identify a reproducible `loose_orbit` cardiac family, but they do not justify a unitary `rigid_orbit` class. The positive result is therefore not a universal three-phenotype taxonomy, but a validated baseline representation that isolates one coherent low-lock family while revealing substantial heterogeneity inside the remaining high-stiffness population. That negative finding is scientifically important rather than merely disappointing, because it indicates that the unresolved cardiac problem now lies in the structure of the non-loose group rather than in the absence of a geometric signal.

The same result also defines a practical translational path. Feature triage reduced the embedded beat-level interface to three primitive quantities, `poincare_b`, `drift_norm`, and `energy_asym`, and fixed-point emulation of the frozen V1 packet preserved exemplar ordering with negligible median absolute error and complete `energy_asym` sign agreement across the benchmark records. Accordingly, the present work yields both a defensible physiological conclusion and a feasible embedded contract: low-bandwidth primitive geometry can be streamed on device, while cohort-level routing, host-side summaries, and any future refinement of the high-stiffness subtypes remain offboard until the residual taxonomy is resolved more fully.
